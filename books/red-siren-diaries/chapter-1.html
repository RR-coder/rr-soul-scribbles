Red Siren Diaries - Chapter 1

The red glow sliced through the foggy Delhi morning as ACP Jaideep Rana zipped his jacket and stepped out of his black Scorpio. Hauz Khas was unusually silent — no honking, no chai stalls open, just the sound of a distant siren echoing down the alley.

At just 26, Jaideep had earned the stars on his shoulder through sheer grit. From a middle-class household, a father in the army who didn’t approve of his police dreams, to finally cracking the UPSC in his third attempt after B.Tech — his journey was never ordinary.

But this case wasn’t either.

A politician’s son found dead in an abandoned ambulance, engine running, siren screaming. And no CCTV footage.

"Sir, no sign of forced entry," said Inspector Mehra, already at the scene.

Jaideep crouched near the ambulance. His sharp eyes scanned the blood-stained stretcher, the perfectly coiled IV tubes, and a small sticker on the dashboard — a saffron lotus with a QR code. That wasn’t hospital standard.

Something was off. This wasn’t a murder out of rage. It was a message.

He called for the cyber team and the forensics. While the body was being moved, Jaideep noticed scratch marks on the victim’s wrist — someone tried to free himself. There was a camera installed in the ambulance, but the footage had been wiped clean. Professionally.

Hours later, the lab results arrived. No sign of drugs or alcohol, but a unique sedative compound was detected — used only by military-grade agencies.

"Sir," the cyber expert said, "That QR code links to an anonymous blog. Last updated just today. The post says: 'Operation Red Siren begins. This is just the first.'"

"Whoever did this," Jaideep muttered, "knows how to use fear as a weapon. And they want to play."

By evening, a minister held a press conference calling it an accident. Jaideep's seniors asked him to step back from the investigation.

He didn’t.

At night, another siren blared across South Delhi. Another ambulance. Another body.

The game had begun.
